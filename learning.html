🔧 Types of Supports in Pressure Vessels: When and Why We Use Them

One of the first things we decide in vessel design is how to support it. The choice affects stresses, stability, and even cost. Here's a quick guide:

1. Saddle Supports (Horizontal Vessels)

When used: Almost always for horizontal vessels like storage tanks, heat exchangers.

Why: They distribute the weight evenly along the shell. Two saddles are standard – one fixed, one sliding to accommodate thermal expansion. Design considerations: saddle angle (typically 120°–150°) affects stress concentration; wear plates may be needed for thin shells.

2. Skirt Supports (Vertical Vessels)

When used: Tall vertical columns, reactors, towers.

Why: Provides stable base, allows access for bottom nozzles, and can accommodate thermal expansion if designed with a flared or straight skirt. Must be checked for wind/seismic loads, and often includes openings for piping and manways.

3. Leg Supports (Small Vertical Vessels)

When used: Small to medium vertical vessels, often shop-fabricated.

Why: Simple, low-cost, easy to install. But legs create local stresses – need reinforcing pads. Height must allow clearance for bottom outlets.

4. Lug Supports (Elevated Vessels)

When used: Vessels mounted on steel structures (like platforms).

Why: Compact, allows welding directly to shell. Lugs can be continuous or intermittent. Must check for local stresses and provide gussets if needed.

💡 Pro tip: In PV Elite, when modeling supports, pay attention to load combinations – especially wind + seismic + operating weight. Always verify local stresses (Zick analysis for saddles, local stress checks for lugs/legs).
