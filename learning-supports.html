1. What do you mean by "support" in static equipment?

Interview answer framework:

A support in static equipment is a structural component that transfers loads from the equipment to the foundation or supporting structure while maintaining stability under all operating conditions.

Follow-up questions they ask:

  • Why do we need supports? – To carry weight, resist external loads (wind, seismic), accommodate thermal expansion, and maintain equipment orientation.
  • What loads are transferred through supports? – Dead load, operating weight, test weight, wind, seismic, thermal forces, and occasional loads like piping thrusts.

💡 Expert tip: Don't just say "it holds the vessel." Explain load transfer, stability, and boundary conditions. Mention that supports define the equipment's restraint system – fixed points guide expansion, sliding points prevent overstress.

2. What are the different types of supports used for pressure vessels?

Classification approach (impresses interviewers):

By orientation:

  • Vertical vessels: Skirt, leg, lug, bracket
  • Horizontal vessels: Saddle supports (typically two)

By equipment type:

  • Towers/columns: Skirt supports (most common)
  • Storage tanks: Saddles (horizontal), legs (small vertical)
  • Heat exchangers: Saddles, brackets, or fixed/sliding arrangements

By load condition:

  • Fixed supports: Restrain movement in all directions (one saddle in a pair is fixed)
  • Sliding supports: Allow thermal expansion (other saddle, PTFE pads, rollers)
  • Guides: Restrain lateral movement but allow axial expansion

⚠️ Trick question they ask: "Why is skirt preferred for tall vertical vessels?"
Answer: Skirts provide uniform load distribution to the foundation, accommodate large thermal gradients, allow access for bottom nozzles, and resist overturning moments from wind/seismic better than legs or lugs.

3. Explain skirt support in detail (with interview traps)

Very common interview question – here's a complete answer:

A skirt support is a cylindrical shell welded to the bottom of a vertical vessel (either to the bottom head or shell) that transfers loads to the foundation.

Key design aspects they drill on:

Why is skirt welded to shell or head?

  • Welded to head: Better stress distribution, avoids interrupting vessel shell, common for tall columns.
  • Welded to shell: Sometimes used for shorter vessels or when head is thin, but creates a stress discontinuity.

Why is a vent hole provided in skirt?

  • To allow trapped gases to escape during welding and heat treatment, preventing pressure buildup.
  • Also allows inspection access and drainage.
  • ⚠️ What happens if vent hole is not provided? During PWHT, trapped air expands and can bulge or rupture the skirt-to-head weld.

How is skirt thickness decided?

  • Based on compressive stress from dead weight + bending from wind/seismic.
  • Also checked for local buckling (especially for large diameters).
  • Typically 8-20 mm depending on vessel size, but must be at least the thickness of attached shell/head per many company standards.

💡 Killer answer: Mention that skirt thickness is often governed by buckling rather than pure compression – so you check both. Also, for high-temperature service, you need to consider the skirt's thermal gradient and may need a thermal sleeve or refractory lining.
